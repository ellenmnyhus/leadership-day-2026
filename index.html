Phase 2 · 60 minutes · Moderators + BonsaiLabs
🏗️ Build a Company Replica — Then Discover What Happened
Groups of 6–8 · Assigned by business model
Participants split into three groups based on business model. Each group is assigned one of three fictional companies (see the company tabs below). With the help of their moderator and BonsaiLabs support, each group builds a simple Claude-powered replica of their assigned company — feeding in the company context, building a basic AI assistant, and exploring what AI could do for that business. Then — after they've built it — they read what actually happened to the company. The post-mortem hits very differently once you've just spent 30 minutes thinking like that leadership team. The final 15 minutes are spent in group discussion, answering the post-mortem questions together.
Why This Sequence Works
Building the replica first creates empathy and investment in the company. Reading what happened afterwards creates a visceral "that could be us" reaction that a pure case study never achieves. The discussion questions then connect directly to the participant's own business — at exactly the moment when the lessons feel most real.

Group Assignment · B2C Companies
Build a replica of NordicHome Group
NordicHome is a Scandinavian online home & lifestyle retailer. €180m revenue, 18 European markets, strong brand among 35–55 year old women. Products: furniture, garden, lighting, decorative items. Sells via own webshop and Amazon/Otto/eBay. Private equity-backed.
1
Open Claude and create a new conversation. Start by describing NordicHome to Claude — its products, customers, markets, and business model — so Claude "becomes" the company context.
2
Ask Claude: "What are the top 5 ways AI could create value or protect this business right now?" Discuss the answers as a group.
3
Pick one use case and build it: e.g. ask Claude to generate AI-powered product descriptions, a customer personalisation strategy, or a marketplace optimisation plan for NordicHome.
4
Now read what actually happened to NordicHome below ↓ and answer the discussion questions as a group.
What Actually Happened — Post Mortem 2028
By early 2028, NordicHome was in administration. Revenue had collapsed from €180m to €94m in three years. The brand had become invisible. The team had shrunk from 280 to 90 people.
2024 — Warning signs dismissed
AI-native competitors emerged offering hyper-personalised recommendations, dynamic pricing undercutting NordicHome by 8–15%, and AI-generated product photography reducing time-to-market from 6 weeks to 48 hours. The board's response: "These are startups. We'll watch and see." No formal AI risk assessment was requested.
2025 — The data they didn't read
Webshop conversion fell from 3.2% to 2.1%. The analytics team flagged it. The marketing director called it "macro headwinds." The report sat unread by the CEO for six weeks. A competitor launched an AI room planner. NordicHome approved a scaled-back version at €400k. It was never completed.
2026–2028 — Talent exodus and collapse
The Head of Data resigned: "The strategy meeting where I showed we were losing share in every age group under 45 — and nothing changed." Amazon delisted two top categories citing insufficient product data. Revenue from Amazon — 38% of total — dropped 40% in a quarter. Administration followed in Q1 2028.
The human dimension
The CEO's identity was the brand. He avoided AI because engaging with it meant confronting the possibility that what he'd built was becoming obsolete. The COO saw the data but stayed silent — "the most expensive silence of my career." The board asked the wrong questions at every meeting.
Discussion Questions
1.You just built an AI tool for NordicHome. How different would the company look if the leadership team had done what you just did — two years ago?
2.At what point did the leadership team have enough information to act — and what stopped them?
3.The COO saw the risk but stayed silent. What would have needed to be different in your own leadership culture for her to speak up and be heard?
4.What is the equivalent "Amazon algorithm change" that could hit your own business — and do you know when it would happen?
Group Assignment · B2B SaaS Companies
Build a replica of Fieldworx AS
Fieldworx is a Nordic B2B SaaS company. €28m ARR, 320 customers in construction, facilities management, and field services. Core product: workforce management software for scheduling, timekeeping, and compliance reporting. Deeply embedded in customer operations. Private equity-backed.
1
Open Claude and describe Fieldworx — its product, customers, market, and how the software is used day-to-day — so Claude has full context.
2
Ask Claude: "How could an AI-native competitor disrupt Fieldworx in the next 2 years — and what should Fieldworx build to defend and grow?"
3
Pick one idea and prototype it: ask Claude to draft a product spec for an AI scheduling agent, or build a simple compliance assistant for a Fieldworx customer.
4
Now read what actually happened to Fieldworx below ↓ and answer the discussion questions as a group.
What Actually Happened — Post Mortem 2028
By 2028, Fieldworx had been sold for €18m — less than half its 2024 valuation. ARR had fallen to €17m. Annual churn had risen from 4% to 31%.
2024 — The right question dropped
Three AI-native startups raised a combined €180m to build agentic workforce management tools. At Fieldworx, the 2025 roadmap focused on UI improvements. One board member asked about AI. The CEO said they were "monitoring developments." The question was not revisited.
2025 — Customers start asking
Three large customers asked about AI capabilities during renewals. They had seen demos from Autonoma — an AI agent reducing admin time by 60%. Fieldworx's response: "Their product is impressive but immature. Our customers won't switch." Two customers renewed at 15% discounts. The third did not renew — the first competitive loss in four years.
2026–2028 — The cascade
The CTO resigned: "We are building the wrong things." Recruiting a replacement took 7 months. In Q1 2027, seven enterprise customers — €6m ARR — did not renew, all citing AI. A bridge round failed. The company sold for €18m in February 2028.
The human dimension
"Our customers aren't asking for it" was a trap — they didn't know what to ask for. The CTO raised warnings three times and was not heard. Nobody owned AI. "Monitoring developments" meant nobody was responsible for turning it into action.
Discussion Questions
1.You just prototyped an AI feature for Fieldworx in 15 minutes. How does that change your view of what the product team should have been building?
2.The CTO raised concerns three times and was not heard. What does that tell you about decision-making culture — and whose responsibility was it to fix it?
3."Our customers aren't asking for it" — when is this ever a valid reason not to act on a technology shift?
4.What is the equivalent of Autonoma in your market — and what would your customers say if a competitor showed them a demo tomorrow?
Group Assignment · Decarb / Industrial Services Companies
Build a replica of ClearVolt Energy Services
ClearVolt provides energy efficiency and building decarbonisation services for commercial real estate and industrial clients. €45m revenue, 180 staff across Norway, Germany and Netherlands. Services: energy audits, retrofit planning, on-site installation oversight, ongoing monitoring. Human experts at the centre of every engagement. Private equity-backed.
1
Open Claude and describe ClearVolt — its services, clients, delivery model, and the role of human expertise — so Claude has full context.
2
Ask Claude: "Where in ClearVolt's business could AI replace or enhance human-led processes — and where does the human remain irreplaceable?"
3
Build something: ask Claude to design an AI-powered continuous monitoring report for a commercial building, or draft a client proposal for an "energy-as-a-service" model.
4
Now read what actually happened to ClearVolt below ↓ and answer the discussion questions as a group.
What Actually Happened — Post Mortem 2028
By 2028, ClearVolt was acquired in a distressed process for €22m — against a 2024 enterprise value of €40m. Margins had collapsed from 18% to 4%.
2024–2025 — Winning on trust, losing on economics
AI-powered energy management platforms from the US and UK entered Europe. Their model: AI analysed building data remotely, generated retrofit recommendations automatically, monitored performance in real time — at a fraction of ClearVolt's cost. ClearVolt's NPS was 72. The CEO's view: "You can't automate a conversation with a building manager who's run the same facility for 20 years." No formal response was developed.
2026 — The wrong scale
Customers used AI competitor proposals as leverage. Gross margin fell from 18% to 11%. The board approved a €3.5m technology platform investment — then scaled it back to €1.2m to protect short-term EBITDA. Development began with two engineers.
2027–2028 — GridMind arrives
US competitor GridMind, backed by €120m, entered Germany and signed ClearVolt's three largest German customers — €8m ARR — on multi-year contracts. Acquired February 2028. The acquiring CEO: "They waited too long to change the model, and when they did try, they underinvested by a factor of ten."
The human dimension
The CEO's identity was built around human expertise. "Customers love us" masked the structural problem — NPS doesn't tell you which customers are looking at competitor demos. The board approved the right solution at the wrong scale, optimising for the wrong time horizon. Nobody on the board had technology or AI expertise.
Discussion Questions
1.You just built an AI monitoring report for ClearVolt in 10 minutes. How does that change the argument that "you can't automate human expertise"?
2.The board approved €1.2m instead of €3.5m to protect short-term EBITDA. How do you make better capital allocation decisions when choosing between margin protection and survival?
3.Nobody on ClearVolt's board had technology or AI expertise. What does that tell you about board composition — and does it apply to your own board?
4.Where in your own business is the "human expertise" argument being used to avoid engaging with AI — and is it valid?

Breakout · 45 minutes · Sascha Alber
AI Resilience & Opportunity Tool
Sascha introduces Verdane's proprietary framework. Participants score their own company, discuss in groups, and identify the one action they will take. 45 minutes — focused and practical.
Session Format · 45 min
Intro · Score · Discuss · Read-back
Sascha Alber · CEO & COB tracks separately
Sascha introduces the framework in 5 minutes — explaining the two tiers and what the score means. Participants self-score silently for 10 minutes. Groups of 4–5 discuss for 20 minutes. Final 10 minutes: brief read-back from 2–3 groups, individual written commitment. The tool measures both resilience (how protected is the business from AI disruption?) and opportunity (where can AI create the most value?). A low score is not a verdict — it's a starting point.
Intended Purpose
Give every leader a structured, honest baseline — not as a theoretical exercise, but as the starting point for a real conversation with their board and leadership team in the week after Leadership Day.
Tier 1 — Structural Moats
Yes / No Questions
If any YES → company scores 7–8 / 8 on resilience
T1.1 — Is value delivered through integrated and proprietary hardware AND software? T1.2 — Does the company benefit from strong network effects? T1.3 — Does the company deliver differentiated physical product value that AI cannot bypass with software alone?
Tier 2 — Supporting Moats
Scored 1–8 Each
Two or more at 7+ indicates strong resilience
T2.1 — Does the product create an audit trail regulators and auditors rely on? T2.2 — Does the product encode expertise that takes years to build? T2.3 — Is the product the system of record? T2.4 — Would operations stop without this product? T2.5 — Does the company have a strong brand and a direct contractual relationship with customers? Score interpretation: • 7–8 / 8 — Structurally resilient. AI is more opportunity than threat in the near term. • 5–6 / 8 — Moderate resilience. Active AI strategy needed now. • 1–4 / 8 — Vulnerable. Immediate action required on both defence and offence.
Discussion Questions · Groups of 4–5
What Does Your Score Tell You?
1. Share your score and the one dimension you found hardest to answer — why? 2. Where in your business is AI most likely to create value in the next 12 months? 3. What is the one structural thing you could do to improve your resilience score in 2 years? For COBs: replace question 3 with — "What governance or board-level decision does your score suggest you should be having with your CEO?"
