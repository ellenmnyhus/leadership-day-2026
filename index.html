Breakout Option A
AI Resilience Scoring
Participants score their own company against Verdane's proprietary AI Resilience Framework and identify their single biggest vulnerability. A practical, structured starting point for real leadership decisions.
Purpose & Format
Why This Session
CEO track: Ellen + Caroline · COB track: Niklas + Sascha · 75 min
Every CEO and COB leaves with an honest, structured view of where their company stands on AI resilience — not as a theoretical exercise, but as a practical starting point for leadership decisions. The session uses Verdane's proprietary AI Resilience Framework, scored on a 1–8 scale. Tier 1 contains three yes/no structural moat questions. If any Tier 1 answer is YES, the company is highly resilient (score 7–8). Tier 2 scores five supporting moats on the same 1–8 scale.
Intended Purpose
Give every leader an honest baseline. A low score isn't a verdict — it's a starting point. The session surfaces the one structural action each company should prioritise, and creates individual written commitments that Verdane follows up on within two weeks.
The Framework
Tier 1 — Structural Moats
Yes / No questions — if any YES, company scores 7–8 / 8
T1.1 — Is value delivered through integrated and proprietary hardware AND software? T1.2 — Does the company benefit from strong network effects? T1.3 — Does the company deliver differentiated physical product value that AI cannot bypass with software alone? A single Tier 1 YES indicates strong structural AI resilience. The company's core value delivery cannot be replicated by an AI-native competitor without years of capital investment.
The Framework
Tier 2 — Supporting Moats
Scored 1–8 each · two or more at 7+ indicates strong resilience
T2.1 — Does the product create an audit trail regulators and auditors rely on? T2.2 — Does the product encode expertise that takes years to build? T2.3 — Is the product the system of record? T2.4 — Would operations stop without this product? T2.5 — Does the company have a strong brand and a direct contractual relationship with its customers? Score interpretation: • 7–8 / 8 — Structurally resilient. AI is more opportunity than threat in the near term. • 5–6 / 8 — Moderate resilience. Some protection but active AI strategy needed now. • 1–4 / 8 — Vulnerable. Immediate action required on both defence and offence.
Session Structure · 75 minutes
How the Session Runs
Introduction (10 min) — Facilitator intro, hand out printed scorecards, explain the two tiers. Key framing: "There is no right answer. A low score isn't a verdict — it's a starting point." Individual self-scoring (10 min) — Each participant scores their company in silence across all 8 dimensions. Small group discussion (40 min) — Groups of 4–5, pre-mixed across sectors. Three questions: 1. Share your score and the one dimension you found hardest to answer — why? 2. Where in your business is AI most likely to create value in the next 12 months? 3. What is the one structural thing you could do to improve your resilience score over the next 2 years? Share-back (10 min) — 2–3 groups share key insights. Facilitator draws out patterns. Individual call to action (5 min) — Each participant writes: "The one thing I will do in my company as a result of today."
COB Track Note
For COBs, redirect discussion question 3 to: "What governance or board-level decision does your score suggest you should be having?" The question is less about operational action and more about asking the CEO the right questions.
Breakout Option C
The Human Edge
What will still be human at the top? When AI handles the analysis, the data, and the strategy — what is your irreplaceable leadership contribution? Erik Salvador opens with a short keynote, then three provocations drive group discussion.
Session Format
How This Session Runs
Erik Salvador · CEO & COB tracks together · 75 min
Erik opens with a 10–15 minute keynote drawing on McKinsey, BCG, Deloitte and Harvard research to frame the central question: when AI democratises analytical capacity, what makes a leader irreplaceable? This flows directly into three sharp provocations discussed in groups of 5–6. Each provocation has a research stat to anchor it, a core question, and discussion prompts. No slides for the discussion — just the provocation cards and honest conversation. Closes with a room-wide share-back and individual written commitments: "The one thing I will do differently as a leader in the next 30 days."
Intended Purpose
Make the AI disruption debate personal. Not "what should our company do" — but "what does this mean for me, as a leader, right now?" This session works especially well after the hackathon: participants have just built something with AI, and now the question shifts from "what can AI do?" to "what does that mean for how I lead?"
01
Your leadership advantage just became available to everyone.
92% of companies plan to increase AI investment. Only 1% consider themselves truly ready. — McKinsey
AI democratises analytical capacity. The data, the market insight, the strategic analysis that used to make the best leaders stand out — AI now gives that to everyone. CEOs and CTOs already drive 44.5% of all AI decisions in their organisations. The bottleneck is not technology. It is leadership.
"What does your leadership team offer that AI cannot replicate — and are you all agreed on the answer?"
What is the one thing you personally bring to your organisation that AI genuinely cannot substitute?
Is your leadership team aligned on this — or does everyone have a different answer?
What does your answer mean for how you spend your time?
02
Your organisation is using AI. Your frontline isn't.
75% of senior leaders use AI weekly. Only 51% of frontline employees do. — BCG "Silicon Ceiling"
When employees lack approved tools, 54% find alternatives on their own — creating security risks and chaos. Positive attitudes toward AI jump from 15% to 55% when leaders actively endorse it. Frontline managers are 3× more worried about AI than senior leaders. What looks like resistance is usually a leadership gap: unclear communication, lack of psychological safety, no sense of purpose in the change.
"Are you personally leading AI transformation — or delegating it and hoping it happens?"
What is the gap between how you use AI and how your team uses it?
What would it take for your frontline managers to feel excited rather than threatened?
What have you personally stopped doing because AI does it better — and have you been honest about that with your team?
03
Would you let an AI represent you in a board meeting?
Only 1 in 5 companies has mature governance for autonomous AI agents. — Deloitte 2026
Digital "agentic twins" — autonomous AI that acts on your behalf — are no longer science fiction. They are here. Nearly half of Fortune 100 companies now mention AI risk in board oversight reports — a 3× increase in one year. 136 AI-related laws were passed across US states in 2025 alone. The question is not whether AI will act on your behalf. It already does. The question is how far you are willing to let it go — and who is accountable when it gets it wrong.
"Where is your personal boundary — and does your board know where the company's boundary is?"
Where in your organisation are AI agents already making decisions — and do you know where all of them are?
What is on your board agenda about AI governance — and is it enough?
If your AI made a decision that harmed a customer or employee, who is responsible?
The Underlying Question · Harvard Business School
Change Fitness
HBS defines "change fitness" as the capacity to absorb significant and sustained change. They argue it is the meta-skill that determines whether leaders and organisations succeed or stagnate. The minimum bar: every leader needs at least a 30% digital and AI mindset — not technical expertise, but genuine curiosity and willingness to engage. Warning: when AI takes over tasks that gave people meaning, work risks becoming less meaningful. Leaders must shift from optimising processes to designing them.
Closing Question for the Room
"On a scale of 1–10 — honestly — how change-fit are you as a leader right now? Not your company. You. And what does your organisation need from you in the next 12 months that only a human can provide?"
